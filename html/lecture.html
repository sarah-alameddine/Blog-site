Benford's Law - How mathematics can detect fraud!

So this was mentioned in the chat by ryanb in response to how to do politician question (midterm 2017 q5).
I looked it up and it's pretty interesting math.

If you were looking at a newspaper and you listed all the numbers that appeared on the front page,
most would assume that the number 1 would appear as the first digit 11% of the time (i.e., one of nine possible numbers).

However, it was found that numbers starting with a 1 will turned up 30% of the time.
This is true for things like addresses, populations, constants etc.
As you go up in numbers, this percentage would decrease, as shown in the table below:

	

Note: If your choices are too random or too restricted this law wouldn't be true,
An example of this is looking at height of people.

Now how does this relate to the six politicians question i.e. fraud detection.?
If you look at the first point, I wrote that most people won't realise that 1 is a dominant
starting number in in data sets. So if you calculate the frequency of the of the different
politicians first digit, the ones with 1 occurring more times (or around 30%) would be
the ones more honest about their  expense claims.

 confidentially using an asymmetric cipher, how many distinct private keys would be needed?
 Note: only enter DIGITS in your answer (or else the automarker will mark it wrong.)

ANSWER: 100 - Some notes on Asymmetric cryptography:

It uses public and private keys to encrypt and decrypt data.
The keys are large numbers that have been paired together but are not
identical (asymmetric).
One key in the pair can be shared with everyone; it is
called the public key. The other key in the pair is kept secret; it is called
the private key. Either of the keys can be used to encrypt a message; the opposite
key from the one used to encrypt the message is used for decryption. 

asymmetric encryption are designed to provide confidentiality, integrity & authenticity (Digital Signatures)

source:
https://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/definition/asymmetric-cryptography

TGPRGWTADEKI HI3OYNODONAT ES4LOCIINTB} FC4LURSDTHO_ LO1IRYAEEIU_ AM{NOPBAVNT_

3441{OLLINYOURONCRYPOISABDIDEAONTEVNTHINABOUTT}___EFLATSCOMG

  So I was able to solve 2 more substitution ciphers today and what has really
  helped was following this method:

  (1) Find the most frequent letters, do this by literally counting each letter and
  finding the two most frequent letters - you can use this to guess "e" and "t"

  (2) look for "the" - popular word so try looking for it especially since we would
  have "e" and "t" from above step

  (3) look for double letters eg "ee", "ii"
